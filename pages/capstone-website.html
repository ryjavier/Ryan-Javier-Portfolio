CS & Engineering Dept.
Tools Figma
Type Website Redesign
UI/UX Design

About

The Lehigh University Computer Science Capstone website serves students, faculty, and sponsors navigating the senior capstone program. The existing site had accumulated years of structural debt: inconsistent navigation, buried project content, and a visual identity that didn't reflect the program's scope. This project set out to redesign it from the ground up, improving discoverability and presenting the work students produce with the clarity it deserves.

Before moving into Figma, the layout was mapped through thumbnail sketches to establish the information architecture. Key decisions made at this stage: a persistent top navigation separating About, Projects, Sponsors, and FAQs; a hero section leading with program identity; and a card-based grid for projects to enable browsing and filtering.

Thumbnail Sketches

Mapping structure before touching the screen

Rough layouts established the page hierarchy across all four main sections. The most critical decision was surfacing the Projects page as the primary destination, since that's the content most visitors are seeking.

Every screen in the redesign traces back to a specific friction point from the old site. The comparisons below show exactly what changed and why.

Hero Landing

Before
After

The old landing page carried an outdated visual style and a navbar that wasn't immediately clear to navigate. The photography choice didn't reinforce the program's identity, and the navigation assumed familiarity rather than orienting first-time visitors.

The redesign addresses this directly: a cleaner navbar assumes its users are students first, structuring the experience around their primary goals. The homepage opens with a full campus photo, matching the visual tone visitors already expect from academic department sites. It establishes the program's identity before they read a single word.

A polished front page wasn't just an aesthetic goal. The site's primary audience is prospective students, so the homepage needed to make the program feel worth joining, not just worth reading about.

Faculty

Before
After

The old faculty section forced users to click through each person one at a time, taking up significant space while hiding the information visitors actually needed. The yellow card styling added visual noise without adding clarity.

The redesign shows them all at once with no clicking required. It's cleaner: the yellow is gone, and all faculty names, titles, and contact details are now displayed as a plain visible list. The old site tucked each person behind a card that required its own click to open, turning a simple browse into a repetitive multi-step task.

Projects

Before
After

The old projects page took up too much space, had no way to browse, and didn't distinguish between past and current projects. My redesign fixes that with a search bar and tags to easily search through. Each project now appears as a card showing the team name, sponsor, year, and project category. A visitor can tell at a glance whether a project is relevant to them, with no clicking required. The page also introduces search and filter controls so users can narrow the archive by sponsor, topic, or year without scrolling through everything.

Coming into this project with no prior knowledge of the capstone program, I found myself wanting a way to browse projects without reading through everything. That personal frustration became the direct prompt for building the search function.

Add-on · Quick Access Buttons

Jump to what you need, immediately

Prominent buttons sit directly below the hero so any visitor, whether a student, sponsor, or recruiter, can jump straight to the content they came for without scrolling through the page first.

FAQs

Before
After

The old FAQ had no affordances that told users they could click it. No chevron, no button and a basic hover effect that poorly indicates that the collapsed headings are interactive. Users who don't already know to click them will never discover the content inside.

Every accordion item now carries a chevron that rotates on expand, giving users a clear visual signal that more content is inside. The sponsor pitch and the FAQ list are separated into distinct sections so users can find what they came for without reading through content aimed at a different audience. Contact details are pulled to the top rather than buried at the bottom.

The accordion pattern wasn't a stretch, it was the obvious choice. It matches a familiar interaction model users already understand, and the color choices were pulled directly from Lehigh's branding so the design feels native to the institution.

Timeline

Before
After

The Students and Sponsors pages used the same layout, the same content density, and nearly the same language. A visitor switching between them would struggle to identify what applied to them. The timeline also wasn't effective at conveying information: the structure didn't guide users through the program sequence in a way that was easy to follow. My redesign addresses these directly.

Fixes · Students & Sponsors
The two pages were structural twins, so the fix was architectural rather than visual. Student-relevant content, including program requirements, timeline, and team formation, was consolidated into the homepage About section where it reaches the right audience without needing its own page. The Sponsors page was then scoped entirely to the industry pitch and partnership details, giving each audience a destination that feels written specifically for them.

4 pain points directly addressed with specific design decisions
1 unified visual language replacing years of inconsistent additions and the wireframe section
